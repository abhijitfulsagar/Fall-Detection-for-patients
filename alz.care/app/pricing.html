Frequently Asked Questions

Is FindMe the same as generic GPS locators?

The answer is most definitely, “No.” FindMe is as different from a generic GPS locator as a Boeing 747 is from a Hang Glider.

Basically, FindMe will tell if your loved one is in trouble, and if so, show you where they are. A general purpose GPS Locator will tell you where they are, but not if they’re in trouble.

Is FindMe a “wearable” like a wrist band or an amulet?

We considered making FindMe to be worn as a wrist band, but learned that wrist bands are quickly removed from individuals diagnosed with Alzheimer’s. And any kind of locking mechanism promised to be the source of agitation and worrying, as well has risking having the person with Alzheimer’s feeling stigmatized. Similarly, our research in assisted care facilities showed that amulets worn by residents were constantly being removed. FindMe is made to carry on a key ring, like a key fob, because we found that taking your house keys with one is a deeply ingrained behavior.

When is the right time to think about using FindMe?

We have pretty strong opinions about this question, which are probably not shared by all. We think that the time to start wearing a seat belt is when you start riding in a car, and the time to start using FindMe is when your loved on is diagnosed with Alzheimer’s. We have received messages from very well meaning individuals, who commented that they felt lucky that their loved one had not started wandering yet; but we don’t think that waiting to use FindMe until the onset of wandering is any more wise than waiting to start using a seat belt until after you get into a car accident.

Who does FindMe serve best?

We created FindMe for people diagnosed with Alzheimer’s, and we think it’s most appropriate for those in the early- to mid- stages of Alzheimer’s, who are ambulatory.

How does the Community help you with FindMe?

FindMe allows members of the community, with the caregiver’s permission, to take part in receiving any notifications of hazards that the caregiver wishes to share with them. For example, should a loved one with Alzheimer’s find themselves outdoors in severe, cold weather while their caregiver is many miles away, the community can be notified to act on the caregiver’s behalf in returning the endangered person to safety.
